what LLaMA knows about Arabic morphology (and won't say)

probing internal representations across model scales
mohammed al-thobaiti · 2026-05-26
Arabic NLP probing morphology ember LLaMA scaling

motivation

the Alakeel et al. (2026) paper left an open question: GPT-4o gets 97% on nonce Arabic root-pattern generation despite having the worst tokenizer alignment in the study (17% boundary precision). ALLAM, with the best tokenizer alignment, collapses to 20% on the same task. tokenizer quality doesn't predict morphological competence , so what does?

the authors suggested "compositional reasoning + instruction-following" as the mechanism. but that's a behavioral description. this experiment asks the mechanistic question: what do the internal representations actually look like?

i used ember, a from-scratch inference engine that gives direct access to hidden states at every transformer block, to probe LLaMA 3.2 1B, LLaMA 3.2 3B, and LLaMA 3.1 8B on a nonce root-pattern task. 20 invented triliteral roots (from the Alakeel dataset) crossed with 10 Arabic morphological patterns = 200 stimuli, all Latin-transliterated for ASCII-safe probing. four analysis methods:

  • linear probes , per-layer logistic regression: is root identity (20-way) or pattern identity (10-way) linearly decodable from hidden states?
  • CCA , canonical correlation analysis: how similar are representations across layers? are root and pattern probe weights disentangled?
  • RSA , representational similarity analysis: do layers organize stimuli the same way geometrically?
  • divergence , correct-vs-incorrect: at which layer do hidden states for correct and incorrect predictions separate?

a note on method: probes measure what information is recoverable from hidden states, not what the model causally uses during generation. linear separability is a lower bound on the structure present; it doesn't imply the model relies on that structure for its outputs.

Observation

all three models output "The" for every single stimulus. 0/200 correct across every scale. the morphological information is recoverable, structured, and appears separable under the probe setup , but they never reach the output.

the pipeline

generate_stimuli.py        → stimuli/nonce_root_pattern.json  (200 stimuli)
ember --probe              → activations.npy + correctness.json
train_linear_probe.py      → probes.npz           (root + pattern accuracy per layer)
cca_analysis.py            → cca.npz              (layer similarity + disentanglement)
rsa_analysis.py            → rsa.npz              (representational similarity)
divergence_analysis.py     → divergence.npz        (correct vs incorrect gap)
plot_results.py --dark     → probe_results.png     (3×2 figure)

all code is in the ember repository. the 1B and 3B models ran on a 16 GB laptop; the 8B required a cloud instance (Hetzner CPX62) for the activation extraction pass. Q8_0 quantization keeps the 3B model under 4 GB RAM and the 8B under 8 GB.

results

LLaMA 3.2 1B (16 layers, 2048-dim hidden)

root identity is trivially decodable at every layer (92–100% accuracy, flat curve). root information remains linearly recoverable across the full depth of the model.

pattern identity shows a clean acquisition curve: 73% at layer 0, jumps to 92% at layer 1, 97% at layer 2, then saturates at 100% from layer 3 onward. pattern becomes recoverable within the first three transformer blocks.

CCA self-similarity starts at 0.92 (layer 0) and saturates rapidly (0.98 by layer 1, 0.999 by layer 4). representations stabilize fast. RSA reveals a sharp representational reorganization at layers 3–4: the next-neighbor correlation drops to 0.46, then recovers , a sharp drop in geometric similarity coinciding with when pattern becomes recoverable.

root-pattern subspace CCA declines from 0.25 to 0.10 across layers , the representations become progressively more separable under the probe setup with depth.

LLaMA 3.2 3B (28 layers, 3072-dim hidden)

this is where things get strange.

root identity develops a U-shaped dip. it starts at 99%, peaks at 100% (layers 2–3), then erodes across mid-layers: 94% → 90% → bottoms at 78% at layer 18–19, before partially recovering to 82% in the final layers. root identity becomes less linearly accessible in mid-layers before partially returning in the deepest blocks.

pattern identity is dead flat at 100% for all 28 layers. pattern is linearly separable as a surface-level feature , no depth required to separate the 10 pattern classes linearly. the 3B model shows pattern identity on a linearly accessible subspace from the first layer onward.

CCA self-similarity starts at 0.889 at layer 0 (slightly lower than 1B's 0.921) but stabilizes faster: 0.977 by layer 1, 0.995 by layer 3. the heatmap is essentially uniform bright yellow from layer 4 onward. RSA shows smooth, continuous transitions with no sharp break like 1B's layer 3–4 drop.

root-pattern CCA is the lowest subspace overlap of all three models: 0.02–0.07 across all layers, minimum 0.021 at layer 1. the probe weight subspaces for root and pattern appear nearly orthogonal under CCA at every layer.

Observation

3B is an anomalous regime. 1B and 8B both show pattern acquisition curves (rising from 69–73% to 100%). 3B is flat 100%. 1B and 8B both have ~0.21–0.25 root-pattern CCA at early layers. 3B starts at 0.07. something about the 3B capacity/depth ratio produces a qualitatively different representational organization , and the model has no Arabic output to show for it.

LLaMA 3.1 8B (32 layers, 4096-dim hidden)

root identity deepens the U-dip: after a brief peak at 100% (layer 1), it descends through 94% → 88% → 75% → bottoms at 70% at layer 19, partially recovers to 89%, then drops again to 79% at the final layer. the curve is textured, not a clean U , jagged descent with a secondary dip at the end. bigger models make root identity progressively harder to linearly decode in mid-layers.

pattern identity returns to a staircase: 69% at layer 0 → 82% → 92% → 100% by layer 3. compressed but present , more like 1B than 3B.

CCA starts dramatically lower: 0.566 at layer 0 , the noisiest early representation of any model. then climbs: 0.68 (layer 1) → 0.94 (layer 2) → 0.99 (layer 4). the heatmap has a visibly dark bottom-left corner that brightens across the first 4 layers. RSA layer 0→1 correlation is 0.76, then smooth 0.96–0.999 thereafter.

root-pattern CCA shows the most interesting curve: it rises to a peak of 0.345 at layer 4 , the probe subspaces briefly show higher overlap , then steadily declines to 0.078 at layer 30. this is a pattern consistent with early representational mixing followed by later separability: the first few layers show increased subspace overlap between root and pattern probe weights, while deeper layers show increasingly separable organization. neither 1B (monotonic decline) nor 3B (flat-near-zero) shows this two-phase pattern.

Key Finding

root probing is non-monotonic with scale. 1B has flat-high root accuracy. 3B develops a U-dip (78% trough). 8B deepens it further (70% trough). root identity becomes less linearly accessible as models get larger , the information is presumably still there but organized in a format that linear probes cannot fully recover from mid-layer states.

discussion

1. the representation–behavior gap

this is the central puzzle. all three models have linearly decodable morphological structure , root identity is classifiable at 70–100% accuracy everywhere, pattern at 100% by layer 3 or sooner. the information is recoverable, structured, and shows high linear separability (subspace CCA < 0.1 in deep layers).

yet every model outputs "The" for every stimulus. the morphological information is recoverable from hidden states but never reaches the output tokens.

this narrows the Alakeel et al. question considerably. the failure mode is not "the model lacks recoverable morphological structure." it's "whatever structure is present doesn't appear to drive the model's autoregressive outputs under these prompts." GPT-4o's 97% accuracy on the same task, with the same (or worse) tokenizer, suggests the difference may be in instruction-tuning: how strongly the representation-to-output pathway is shaped during training.

the Alakeel paper concluded "compositional reasoning + instruction-following substitutes for explicit morphological parsing." our results are consistent with a refinement: instruction-following may be what connects recoverable morphological structure to output behavior. the structure is recoverable without GPT-4o-level tuning; the behavior is not.

2. non-monotonic scaling

"bigger is better" doesn't describe root probing. the accuracy curve goes from flat (1B) to U-shaped (3B) to more deeply U-shaped (8B). this is consistent with the hypothesis from the original research plan:

"if the inflection point is between 1B and 3B, this means explicit morphological injection might only help below ~1B parameters. above that, the model may develop the capability internally , but possibly in a form that linear probes can't access."

the data suggests the inflection point isn't about whether morphological structure is recoverable, but how it is organized. at 1B, root identity is linearly accessible at every layer. at 3B+, root identity appears distributed or transformed in mid-layers into a format that logistic regression can't recover , but the information may still be present (since accuracy partially recovers in deeper layers). this looks like a qualitative change in representational organization, not a failure of the model to capture the distinction.

3. pattern trivialization at 3B

the most dramatic scale effect: pattern requires 3 layers of computation at both 1B and 8B, but zero layers at 3B. this is not a smooth trend , 1B → 3B → 8B at layer 0 is 73% → 100% → 69%. the 3B model is an outlier.

possible explanations:

  1. architectural regime. 3B has 28 layers with 3072-dim hidden states. the capacity-to-depth ratio may create a regime where surface features saturate early layers in a way that doesn't happen at 16L/2048d (1B) or 32L/4096d (8B).
  2. training data artifact. 3B may have seen more transliterated Arabic or morphological pattern-description data during pretraining, making pattern recognition a trivial surface operation.
  3. representational trade-off. when pattern is linearly accessible at every layer, root identity may be organized in a more distributed, non-linear format , which could produce the U-dip if linear probes can't access it. the two patterns may be related.

4. mixing-then-separating at 8B

the 8B root-pattern CCA curve (rise to 0.345 at layer 4, then decline to 0.078) shows a pattern consistent with two-phase organization: early layers show increased subspace overlap between root and pattern probe weights, while deeper layers show increasingly separable subspaces. this hump-and-decline shape is absent at both smaller scales.

if this pattern replicates across model families (Qwen, Mistral), it could be a signature of compositional morphological organization that emerges at larger scale , an intermediate format with higher probe subspace overlap, followed by more separable organization in deeper layers. interestingly, this mirrors how humans process non-concatenative morphology: the root and pattern are inseparable in the surface form and only become distinct at a more abstract level.

caveats

Caveat 1

one model family. these results are LLaMA 3.2/3.1 only. all observations are potentially specific to this architecture, training data, and tokenizer. we don't know if the U-shaped root dip is a LLaMA architectural artifact (RoPE + GQA + SwiGLU + specific training data mix) or a general property of Transformer scaling. a Qwen or Mistral run at comparable sizes would test this in a day of compute.

Caveat 2

prompt format. all current data uses English zero-shot prompts. the "The" output might be a chat-template artifact , LLaMA may be defaulting to an English continuation because it doesn't recognize the morphological instruction format. testing Arabic prompts, one-shot prompts, and different output constraints could change the story. if LLaMA produces actual transliterated Arabic with a better prompt, divergence analysis becomes possible and the representation–behavior gap may narrow.

Caveat 3

sample size. 200 stimuli for 20-way classification is 10 samples per class before cross-validation. the probes may be underpowered in the mid-layer trough. auto fold-reduction helps, but more stimuli or repeated forward passes with different seeds would tighten error bars. the U-dip could partially be a sample-size artifact , though its consistency across 3B and 8B (and deepening with scale) argues against this.

Caveat 4

no non-linear probes yet. the most interesting claim , that root identity becomes organized in a format linear probes can't access in mid-layers , is currently an interpretation, not a direct measurement. a 2-layer MLP probe would test it directly. if MLP probes recover 95%+ accuracy in the 70–78% trough, it's a second strong finding. if they don't, the information might genuinely be attenuated, which is a different (and still interesting) story.

next steps

immediate

  1. test non-zero-shot and Arabic prompts. the en_one, ar_zero, and ar_one templates exist in the stimulus set but haven't been run. the Alakeel paper found one-shot helps weaker models and Arabic prompts are worse behaviorally , but the impact on internal representations is unknown.
  2. complete the 8B pipeline. CCA and RSA data is collected; run the full analysis and confirm the mixing-then-separating CCA pattern holds at higher resolution.
  3. non-linear probes. a 2-layer MLP on mid-layer activations. ~20 lines of PyTorch. directly tests whether root identity is organized in a non-linear format vs. genuinely attenuated.

short-term

  1. cross-model CCA. compare representations across scales (1B→3B, 3B→8B). the cca_cross_model function is already implemented. do the models build similar morphological subspaces?
  2. GPT-2 baseline. no Arabic pretraining, English-only BPE tokenizer. serves as a negative control: do representations look qualitatively different without Arabic exposure?
  3. Qwen or Mistral replication. test whether the U-dip and mixing-then-separating patterns are LLaMA-specific or appear across model families.

medium-term

  1. LLaMA 3.3 70B. if compute is available (quantized 70B fits in ~40 GB RAM), does the U-dip continue deepening or does a new strategy emerge?
  2. Arabic-centric models. ALLAM or FANAR, if open-weight versions are accessible. does a model that produces Arabic output (competent on real roots, poor on nonce per the 2026 paper) have the same internal representations?
  3. intervention experiments. if the representation–behavior gap is about instruction-following connecting representations to output, causal interventions (activation patching, steering vectors) could test whether directing attention toward the morphological subspace improves generation. this moves from correlational probing to causal mechanistic analysis.
